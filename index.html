The Problem Space

The system
feeding us is
breaking us.

Canada's urban food systems are caught in a reinforcing feedback loop: the way we produce food drives climate change, and climate change destroys the conditions under which food can be produced.

Food systems account for nearly a third of Canada's greenhouse gas emissions. Cities, where demand is highest, hold almost no local production capacity. Supply chains optimized for efficiency have no redundancy. And the entire apparatus runs on a value system that prioritizes profit over people and growth over soil health.

This is not a technology problem. It is a values and systems problem — one that technology can accelerate solving, if it's pointed in the right direction.

How the research was conducted

The workshop
was the research.

This project was structured as a Major Research Project in a Master of Design program — not a thesis. The Three Horizons workshop was not merely a data collection method; it was the primary research artifact. What emerged from the room — the values, visions, and leverage points — was the contribution.

A literature review on AI, circular economy, and food systems provided context and framing. The workshop synthesized expert knowledge across disciplines into a co-produced vision. The LASERRS framework emerged from affinity mapping that vision's underlying values.

H1 — Horizon One
Status Quo

Mapping the current Canadian food system — its foundational values, defining patterns, key actors, and the outcomes it is producing. What does the system optimize for? Who does it serve? What does it threaten?

H3 — Horizon Three
Desired Future

Co-envisioning a future food system for Canada. What values would it be built on? What would it look like to feed cities in a way that is regenerative, equitable, and resilient? What glimpses of this future exist today?

H2 — Horizon Two
Innovation Space

The transition territory between H1 and H3. H- actions incrementally improve the status quo. H+ actions actively disrupt dominant paradigms and build toward the desired future. Where do AI interventions fit?

What the research found

An emergent
system with
clear leverage
points.

The synthesis of literature review and workshop data revealed a global food system underpinned by capitalist values — and threatened by the cascading effects of the climate change those values are accelerating. Six specific outcomes threaten food system resilience and security, each of which represents a leverage point for strategic AI intervention.

The Emergent System
Three driving forces · Four system actors
Primary Driver
Capitalist Values

Profit maximization, growth, and market efficiency shape the incentives of every actor in the food system — producers, retailers, consumers, and governments. These values prioritize feeding markets over feeding people.

Compounding Driver
Climate Change

Driven in part by food systems themselves, climate change increasingly threatens the infrastructure food systems depend on — arable land, stable weather, water supply. A reinforcing feedback loop with no natural correction mechanism.

Structural Feature
Globalized Supply Chains

Optimized for cost and year-round variety, global supply chains have extended interdependencies to the point of systemic fragility. Shocks anywhere — pandemic, war, drought — propagate everywhere.

Six outcomes threatening food system resilience & security
01
Centralized Production

Food production concentrated in large corporations and distant geographies, reducing community food sovereignty and compounding supply chain risk.

02
Environmental Degradation

Industrial farming practices erode soil health and reduce long-term agricultural viability through monocultures, chemical inputs, and overextraction.

03
Fragile Supply Chains

Just-in-time delivery models and global sourcing create systems with no redundancy — one disruption anywhere cascades everywhere.

04
Systemic Waste

Cosmetic rejection standards, over-production incentives, and post-consumer waste combine to discard 40–50% of global food production.

05
Premiums on Ethical Food

Sustainably and ethically produced food commands prices that exclude lower-income communities, placing a structural "health tax" on poverty.

06
Cascading Climate Impacts

As climate effects compound — disease, conflict, crop failure — the pressure on an already fragile system increases. Without structural change, the feedback loop accelerates.

Strategic AI Leverage Points
Where AI interventions can reverse these outcomes
Decentralized Production

AI-driven automation enabling hydroponic, aquaponic, and vertical farming at community scale — reducing supply chain dependency through local production.

Soil Regeneration

Deep learning systems that advise farmers on crop selection and practices optimized for soil health and carbon sequestration — not yield maximization.

Supply Chain Resilience

Predictive AI anticipating shortages, routing inefficiencies, and climate disruptions before they become crises in urban food access.

Waste Elimination

Computer vision and dynamic pricing tools keeping food in use longer — from farm to retailer to community resource sharing networks.

Food Science Acceleration

AI assisting research scientists in developing scalable, cost-effective alternatives — lab-grown protein, 3D-printed foods — to reduce industrial agriculture's footprint.

Early Warning Systems

Predictive models providing advance notice of extreme weather, crop disease, and geopolitical supply threats — enabling proactive resilience rather than reactive crisis management.

Four years
of friction.

The research was completed in 2022. In the years since, AI has transformed beyond what any of the workshop's AI experts anticipated. Food system disruptions have continued — and in some cases, worsened. Some of what the workshop imagined has started happening. Some of it looks more naive in hindsight. This is an honest accounting.

Validated
Supply chain fragility as a systemic threat

Post-pandemic supply chain disruptions, the war in Ukraine, and climate-driven harvest failures have confirmed the research's central claim. Long, interconnected supply chains are a structural vulnerability, not an optimization problem.

Validated
AI's capabilities in food systems

The AI leverage points identified in 2022 have broadly materialized — precision agriculture, waste reduction through computer vision, predictive supply chain tools. The technology was directionally right. The adoption pace is slower than anticipated.

Evolved
What "AI" means has fundamentally changed

The workshop discussed AI primarily as machine learning algorithms and optimization systems. Since 2022, generative AI has transformed the landscape entirely. The framework's leverage points remain valid, but the tool set available is dramatically different — and raises new governance questions.

Evolved
LASERRS as sensitizing concept, not proven theory

The framework was generated from a single workshop. It has not been empirically tested. Four years on, LASERRS is better understood as a design tool for practitioners than as a validated theoretical contribution. Its utility requires grounded research to substantiate.

Open
How cities actually operationalize food resilience

The research identified what cities should do. What it couldn't address was how municipal actors actually construct, negotiate, and enact food resilience as a governance problem — and what role technological imagination plays in shaping or limiting those constructions. This remains the open question.

New Territory
Technology as imagination, not just tool

What's emerged over four years is a new question: the idea of AI — what municipal actors think it can do — may shape food system decisions as much as AI's actual capabilities. The technological imagination precedes the deployment. This is where the inquiry is heading.
