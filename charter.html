Canadian Urban Food Commons · Municipal Coalition

The Urban Food
Sovereignty Charter

A covenant between cities, communities, and the land they inhabit
Ratified in full · September 2045 · Ottawa, Toronto, Calgary, Vancouver, Montréal
Preamble
In the years following the great disruptions of the 2020s — when supply chains fractured, harvests failed across three continents, and communities first began to understand the true cost of industrial food systems — cities across Canada began an uneven, contested, and ultimately transformative process of reimagining how they feed themselves.

This Charter emerges from that process. It is not a policy document in the traditional sense. It is a covenant — between communities and the land they inhabit, between this generation and those seven generations ahead, between the cities we built and the ecosystems we have too long extracted from.

It was shaped by the accumulated knowledge of Indigenous food keepers whose stewardship of this land predates any government, urban farmers who proved the city could grow, food scientists who asked better questions, community organizers who refused to accept that hunger and abundance could coexist in the same block, and the municipal actors who chose, again and again, the harder and more honest path.

We ratify it not as an arrival, but as a direction.
✦
Article I

On the Right to Food

Access to nutritious, culturally appropriate, and sustainably produced food is a fundamental right of every person living within Canada's cities. No urban resident shall face food insecurity as a consequence of income, geography, neighbourhood design, or the structural failures of markets not organized to serve them.

  • Municipal governments commit to maintaining a minimum of 72 hours of locally-held food supply at all times, independent of national supply chains.
  • No community food infrastructure — growing spaces, distribution networks, seed libraries — shall be privatized, enclosed, or removed from community governance without a binding public referendum.
  • Food affordability shall be measured and reported annually as a public health metric, with enforceable targets set by community food councils.
Article II

On Localism and Food Sovereignty

Communities hold the right to determine how their food is grown, by whom, and according to what values. The concentration of food production and distribution in the hands of entities accountable to no community shall be recognized as a structural threat to urban resilience.

The city is not a place where food arrives. It is a place where food grows — in rooftops, corridors, stations, walls, and the quiet insistence of people who refused to wait.
  • A minimum of 30% of food consumed within city limits shall be produced within those limits or within 200km by 2050, with interim targets reviewed every five years.
  • Zoning law shall treat food-growing infrastructure as essential urban infrastructure, equivalent in standing to transit or water.
  • Equity in food production belongs first to the communities producing it, not to the platforms or corporations distributing it.
Article III

On Sustainability and Natural Harmony

Food systems within Canadian cities shall be designed to regenerate the natural systems upon which they depend. Extraction without renewal is recognized as a debt against future generations, and its continuation is incompatible with this Charter.

We acknowledge that climate change will continue to reshape the conditions under which food is grown in Canada. Future food systems must be adaptive by design — capable of learning from the land as conditions shift, rather than imposing fixed industrial patterns upon it.

  • Soil health shall be treated as public infrastructure, with mandatory reporting on degradation and enforceable remediation requirements.
  • A national urban composting program shall close the loop between food consumption and soil regeneration in all signatory cities by 2048.
  • Water use in food production shall be governed by the principle of minimum viable extraction — taking no more than what allows the source to fully recover.
Article IV

On Ethics, Labour, and Reconciliation

The exploitation of labour, animals, and ecosystems in the production of food is recognized as a founding wound of our current food system — one that cannot be reformed around the edges, but must be deliberately and structurally dismantled.

Indigenous food sovereignty is not a chapter in this Charter. It is its foundation. The food knowledge held by Indigenous peoples across this land represents millennia of sustainable practice, and any just food future must be built in partnership with, and under the guidance of, those communities — never in appropriation of them.

  • The Three Sisters method, the Seven Generations Principle, and other Indigenous agricultural frameworks shall be formally recognized as expert knowledge systems in all municipal food planning processes.
  • Food labelling shall be a transparency instrument, not a marketing one. Full supply chain disclosure — labour conditions, land use, carbon cost — shall be publicly available for all food products sold within signatory cities.
  • Food workers shall be recognized as essential infrastructure workers, with commensurate protections, compensation floors, and participatory rights in the governance of food systems.
Article V

On Technology in Service of Community

Artificial intelligence, automation, and digital infrastructure shall be deployed in food systems only where they demonstrably increase accessibility, reduce waste, regenerate natural systems, or enhance community food sovereignty. Technology serves the Charter. The Charter does not serve technology.

  • AI systems used in municipal food infrastructure shall be open to community audit and governed by community food councils, not by the vendors providing them.
  • Automated production systems — hydroponic, aquaponic, and vertical farming operations — shall be designed with community ownership structures, preventing the re-centralization of food production under new technological forms.
  • Predictive systems for supply chain resilience and waste reduction shall prioritize the needs of lower-income communities first, whose food security is most vulnerable to supply shocks.
  • No technology shall be deployed that reduces the knowledge, agency, or participation of food growers — whether backyard, community, or commercial.

Speculative Design Artifact. This document does not represent an existing policy, organization, or agreement. It was produced in 2022 as part of a Three Horizons workshop exploring the future of Canadian urban food systems, conducted as a Major Research Project in the Master of Design in Strategic Foresight & Innovation program at OCAD University. It represents the Horizon Three vision co-developed by workshop participants — not a prediction of the future, but a design of a direction worth moving toward.
